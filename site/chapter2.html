Part A: IT Governance

Overview of IT Governance

Simple Definition: IT governance is NOT isolated — it is an integral part of enterprise/corporate governance, covering audit, compliance, legal, and risk management.

IT Governance Process (in order):

Set IT objectives → Assess risk to objectives → Establish controls → Monitor performance → Adjust as needed

Key Terminology Note (CISA uses these interchangeably): - GEIT = Governance of Enterprise Information and Technology - EGIT = Enterprise Governance of Information and Technology - ITG = IT Governance

2.1 Laws, Regulations and Industry Standards

Why Laws Matter for IT Governance

Laws affect the entire information life cycle: receipt, processing, storage, transmission, distribution, and destruction of data.

Global Compliance Requirements (3 major areas): 1. Protection of privacy and confidentiality of personal data 2. Intellectual property rights 3. Reliability of financial information

⚠️ EXAM NOTE: CISA does NOT test knowledge of specific laws/regulations. Know the concepts, not the actual law text.

2.1.2 Governance, Risk and Compliance (GRC)

Definition (OCEG): "The integrated collection of capabilities that enable an organization to reliably achieve objectives, address uncertainty and act with integrity — to achieve Principled Performance."

GRC Components: | Component | Definition |-----------|-----------| Governance | Managing the enterprise's policies, processes and decisions | Risk | Identifying, assessing and treating potential risk | Compliance | Adhering to laws, regulations, standards and policies |

GRC Focus Areas: Financial, IT, Legal

Why GRC Exists: Assurance processes cannot exist in silos — they must be integrated.

Assurance functions under GRC include: Internal audit, compliance programs, ERM, ORM (operational risk management), incident management.

2.2 Organizational Structure, IT Governance and IT Strategy

Corporate Governance Basics

Corporate Governance: A system through which enterprises are directed and controlled (Board of Directors is responsible).

Goals of Corporate Governance: - Build environment of trust, transparency, accountability - Foster long-term investment, financial stability, business integrity

2.2.1 Enterprise Governance of Information and Technology (EGIT)

EGIT: All stakeholders (board, senior management, departments) provide input into IT decision-making.

EGIT Responsibility: Board of Directors + Executive Management

Two Core Issues of EGIT: 1. IT delivers VALUE to the business (driven by strategic alignment) 2. IT RISK is managed (driven by accountability)

EGIT Framework Processes (3): | Process | Focus |---------|-------| IT Resource Management | Maintain updated inventory, address risk management | Performance Measurement | Ensure IT resources perform as expected, identify risk early | Compliance Management | Address legal, regulatory, contractual compliance requirements |

COBIT Distinction: Governance vs. Management | Governance | Management |-----------|-----------| Evaluates stakeholder needs | Plans, builds, runs, monitors | Sets direction via prioritization | Executes in alignment with direction | Monitors performance and compliance | Achieves enterprise objectives |

⚠️ KEY EXAM POINT: IT was once just an "enabler" — now it is an integral part of strategy. Strategic alignment between IT and enterprise objectives is a Critical Success Factor (CSF).

2.2.2 Good Practices for EGIT

Why EGIT Has Become Important: - Better ROI demanded from IT investments - Increasing IT expenditure - Regulatory requirements (SOX, Basel Accords, GDPR) - Cloud computing management - Cybersecurity risk - Need for benchmarking - Cost optimization through standardization

EGIT Governance Processes Monitor: - Conformance and performance - System of internal controls - Compliance with external requirements

2.2.3 Audit's Role in EGIT

Audit's Primary Role: Provides assurance that the enterprise takes necessary action to mitigate risk that prevents achieving objectives.

Audit Characteristics: - Operates independently from basic management structure - Reports to applicable oversight body (e.g., audit committee) - Provides leading-practice recommendations to senior management

Audit Charter Requirements for EGIT: - Scope of work (clear definition of functional areas) - Reporting line (to highest level of enterprise) - IS auditor's right of access to information (both internal and third-party)

What IS Auditor Assesses in EGIT: - Alignment between enterprise governance and EGIT - Alignment of IT function with enterprise mission/vision/values - Achievement of performance objectives - Legal, environmental, security, privacy requirements - Control environment - Inherent risk within IS environment - IT investment/expenditure

Three Lines Model (IIA)

GOVERNING BODY (Board) ↓ Accountability
MANAGEMENT
├── FIRST LINE → Provision of products/services; managing risk
├── SECOND LINE → Expertise, support, monitoring, challenge on risk matters
└── THIRD LINE (Audit) → Independent and objective assurance and advice
Line Role Function
First Line Enterprise management Owns and manages risk through controls, metrics, reviews
Second Line Risk management Evaluates risk independently; partners with first line
Third Line Internal Audit Independent testing and assurance; reports to Audit Committee

KEY: Third line (audit) is independent — auditors set their own programs, decide scope independently, have a separate reporting line to the audit committee.

IMPORTANT: Second line may inform and assess management decisions but should NOT replace or overrule them.

2.2.4 Information Security Governance

NIST Definition: "The process of establishing and maintaining a framework and supporting management structure and processes to provide assurance that information security strategies are aligned with and support business objectives, are consistent with applicable laws and regulations through adherence to policies and internal controls, and provide assignment of responsibility, all in an effort to manage risk."

Information Security Governance Framework Elements: 1. Comprehensive security strategy linked with business objectives 2. Governing security policies addressing strategy, controls, and regulation 3. Complete set of standards for each policy 4. Effective security organizational structure (void of conflicts of interest) 5. Institutionalized monitoring processes for compliance and feedback

Primary Outcomes of Effective Information Security Governance: - Strategic alignment - Risk management - Compliance - Value delivery

Enabled by: - Performance measurement — SMART objectives (Specific, Measurable, Attainable, Realistic, Timely) - Resource management — Efficient use of knowledge and infrastructure - Process integration — Integrating fragmented security activities

Why Security Has Become a Governance Issue: - Global networking - Rapid technological innovation - Increased dependence on IT - Sophisticated threat agents/exploits - Extension of enterprise beyond traditional boundaries

CEO → Board Accountability: CEO is accountable to the board for information security governance.

Board-Level Policy Approvers Typically Include: Directors, CEO, COO, CFO, CRO, CIO, CTO, HR head, Chief of Audit, CCO, Legal

2.2.5 Information Systems Strategy

IS strategic processes are integral to enterprise governance to ensure both existing and emerging business goals are achieved as facilitators for competitive advantage.

2.2.6 Strategic Planning

Strategic Planning (IS): Long-term direction for leveraging IT to improve business processes.

Traditional Planning Cycle: Every 3-5 years (now recognized as insufficient)

Agile Strategy / Strategic Agility: Continuous strategic planning with shorter time horizons for faster responses.

Key Considerations for IS Strategic Planning: - Requirements for new and revised IS - IT organization's capacity to deliver - Review of existing system portfolio (functional fit, cost, risk) - Review of technical infrastructure and support processes - ROI on existing IT - Decommissioning of legacy systems - Balance: maintenance cost of existing systems vs. cost of new initiatives

IS Auditor's Role in Strategic Planning: - Ensure IT strategic plans synchronized with overall business strategy - Assess how operational, tactical, business development plans are considered in IT strategy - Check if CIO/senior IT management creates overall business strategy - RED FLAG: Lack of IT involvement in business strategy creation = risk of misalignment

Types of Plans: | Plan Type | Description |-----------|-------------| Long-range | 3-5 years; recognize enterprise goals, technological advances, regulatory requirements | Short-range | Must be integrated with enterprise short-range plans; enables IT agility |

2.2.7 Business Intelligence (BI)

BI Definition: Tools and processes that provide management with data and insights for informed decisions.

What BI Helps Businesses Do: 1. Identify trends and patterns 2. Understand customer behavior 3. Assess performance 4. Make better decisions

Areas Where BI Is Applied: - Process cost, efficiency, quality - Customer satisfaction - Customer profitability - Staff KPI achievement - Risk management (unusual transaction patterns)

Why BI Is a Distinct IT Activity: - Modern enterprises are increasingly complex - Pursuit of competitive advantage - Legal requirements (financial reporting)

Data Architecture Components (for effective BI): 1. Enterprise Data Flow Architecture (EDFA) 2. Logical Data Architecture

IT Governing Committees

IT Steering Committee Primary Functions:

  1. Review IT long- and short-range plans vs. enterprise objectives
  2. Approve major hardware/software acquisitions
  3. Approve/monitor major projects, IS plans, budgets
  4. Review sourcing strategies (insourcing/outsourcing)
  5. Review adequacy of resource allocation
  6. Make centralization vs. decentralization decisions
  7. Support enterprise-wide information security management
  8. Report to board on IS activities

CISO Reporting: CISO may report to CIO or have dotted-line relationship. Potential conflict: CIO's goal = efficient continuous IT services; CISO's goal = quality of protection (may resist cost reduction).

DBA Control Approaches: 1. Separation of Duties (SoD) 2. Management approval of DBA activities 3. Supervisor review of access logs and activities 4. Detective controls over use of database tools

SCADA (Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition): - Centralized systems monitoring/controlling entire sites - Used in industrial plants, power plants, electrical facilities - Remote Terminal Units (RTUs) or PLCs perform site control - Migrating from dedicated lines to Internet — creates cyber attack vulnerability

Separation of Duties (SoD)

Purpose: Prevents a single person from being responsible for diverse critical functions, reducing errors and fraud.

Duties That MUST Be Segregated: 1. Custody of assets 2. Authorization 3. Recording transactions

Consequences of Inadequate SoD: - Misappropriation of assets - Misstated financial statements - Inaccurate financial documentation - Undetected improper use of funds - Unauthorized changes to data and programs

INCOMPATIBLE Roles (Cannot be combined): | Combination | Why Incompatible |-------------|-----------------| Security admin + Change management | Security admin access rights could allow undetected changes | Computer operations + System development | Operator could run self-amended programs | System development + Change management | Modifications could bypass change control approvals | DBA + Access rights granting | Prevents conflicting functions |

COMPATIBLE Roles (CAN be combined): - System development + System maintenance (same programmer; needs dev access but NOT production access)

Authorization should be SEPARATED from: origination, recording, correction

Compensating Controls (When SoD Is Impractical): 1. Audit trails — map transaction flow; allow tracing from origination to update 2. Reconciliation — independent verification using control totals 3. Exception reporting — supervisory handling; require evidence of resolution 4. Transaction logs — manual or automated recording of all transactions 5. Supervisory reviews — observation, inquiry, or remote monitoring 6. Independent reviews — detect errors/irregularities when duties can't be segregated

Remote Logging: Privileged users (DBA, sys admins) should have logs sent to a separate server they cannot access — ensuring log integrity.

SoD Control Matrix: NOT an industry standard — it's a guideline for identifying potential SoD conflicts and compensating controls.

2.2.9 Auditing IT Governance Structure

Red Flags/Warning Signs for IS Auditor: - Excessive costs / budget overruns - Late projects / frequent aborted projects - High staff turnover / inexperienced staff - Frequent HW/SW errors - Excessive backlog of user requests - Slow computer response time - Unsupported or unauthorized HW/SW purchases - Extensive exception reports not followed up - Lack of succession plans - Reliance on key personnel - Lack of adequate training

Documents to Review: - IT strategies, plans, budgets - Security policy documentation - Organization/functional charts - Job descriptions - IT steering committee reports - System development and program change procedures - Operations procedures - HR manuals - QA procedures

2.3 IT Policies, Standards, Procedures and Guidelines

Key Hierarchy: Policies → Standards → Procedures → Guidelines

POLICIES (Constitution — Governance) ↓ Sets the "what" and "why"
STANDARDS (Laws — Management) ↓ Defines boundaries and criteria
PROCEDURES (Operational steps — Operations) ↓ Defines the "how"
GUIDELINES (Suggestions — Operations) ↓ Provides detail and clarification

2.3.1 Policies

Definition: High-level statements of management intent, expectations and direction.

Characteristics: - Can remain static for extended periods in mature enterprises - Are the "constitution" of governance - Must align with and support strategic objectives - Both high-level (enterprise) and lower-level (division/department) exist - Lower-level policies must be consistent with and support high-level policies - Must be reviewed periodically - Should specify who approved and review date

When Policies Must Be Updated: - New technology - Changes in risk and control environment - Significant changes in business processes - Regulatory changes

IS Auditor's Focus on Policies: - Test policies for compliance - Identify policies that hinder business objectives (must report for improvement) - Consider extent policies apply to third parties/OSPs

Information Security Policy (ISP)

ISP: A set of rules and/or statements an enterprise develops to protect information and related technology.

ISP Must Balance: Level of control ↔ Level of productivity (control cost should NOT exceed benefit)

ISP Must Be: - Approved by senior management - Documented and communicated to all employees, service providers, business partners - Referenced against ISO/IEC 27001 (standard) and 27002 (guidelines)

ISP Document Contents: 1. Definition of information security 2. Scope and objectives 3. Statement of management's intent 4. Framework(s) used for risk assessment 5. Compliance requirements (legal, regulatory, contractual) 6. Education, training, awareness requirements 7. Business continuity and disaster recovery plans 8. Consequences of policy violations 9. Responsibilities for IS management 10. References to supplemental documentation

ISP Sub-Policies: - High-level IS policy (CIA statements) - Data classification policy - Acceptable use policy (AUP) - End-user computing policy - Access control policies - Incident response policy - Remote access / WFH policy

ISP Review: - Should have an owner with management responsibility - Reviewed at planned intervals (at least annually) or when significant changes occur - All employees/third parties must sign off annually

Management Review Input (What feeds into ISP review): - Feedback from interested parties - Results of independent reviews - Status of preventive/detective/corrective actions - Previous management review results - Process performance and ISP compliance - Changes in organizational environment - Trends related to threats and vulnerabilities - Reported information security incidents

IS Auditor Reviews Policies For: - Basis/framework used - Appropriateness and completeness - Contents - Exceptions and justifications - Policy approval process - Implementation process - Effectiveness of implementation - Awareness and training - Periodic review and update process

2.3.2 Standards

Definition: Mandatory requirement, code of practice, or specification approved by a recognized external standards body.

Standards Are: The "laws" used to measure policy compliance.

Standards Govern: Creation of procedures and guidelines by setting boundaries (processes, people, technologies).

Professional Standards: Issued by professional organizations (ISACA, IIA) with guidelines and techniques.

Strong standards are essential in fast-moving environments to ensure effectiveness, reliability, trust.

2.3.3 Procedures

Definition: Documented, defined steps for achieving specific policy objectives.

Key Characteristics: - Must be derived from parent policy (reflect spirit of policy) - Must operate within boundaries of standards - Written clearly and concisely - More dynamic than policies — require frequent review - Should be embedded in information systems where possible - Formulated by process owners - IS auditors review procedures to identify controls, evaluate control design, test controls

Problem When Procedures Are Missing/Outdated: - Inconsistent execution - Difficult to identify and assess controls - New employees particularly at risk

2.3.4 Guidelines

Definition: More detailed assistance for following procedure steps.

Guidelines Contain: - Clarification of policies and standards - Dependencies - Suggestions and examples - Background information - Useful tools

Example — System Access (showing hierarchy): | Level | Example |-------|---------| Policy | Information resources must be controlled to prevent unauthorized access | Standard | Principle of least privilege — grant only minimum resources needed for job | Procedure | Accounting department individuals get access to AP system based on job title/role | Guideline | Each accounting team member has a specific role; access is configured to restrict; job changes trigger mandatory manager review; 5-day response window or access is auto-revoked |

2.4 Enterprise Architecture and Considerations

Overview

Enterprise Architecture (EA): Documenting the enterprise's IT assets in a structured manner to facilitate understanding, management, and planning for IT investments.

Purpose: Understand how IT environment is impacted by additional investment; determine ROI/losses.

Concept: IT systems construction is similar to building construction — moving from abstract to physical using models.

Two EA Perspectives: 1. Technology perspective — Clarifies complex technology choices (JavaEE, .NET, cloud, virtualization) 2. Business process perspective — Understands core value-adding processes and supporting technology

2.5 Enterprise Risk Management

Overview

ERM (IT perspective): Process of identifying vulnerabilities and threats to information resources used by an enterprise and deciding what countermeasures to take to reduce risk to an acceptable level (residual risk).

ERM vs. Internal Audit: - ERM = Management function (advises senior leaders on risk strategy) - Internal Audit = Independent audit function

Risk Appetite vs. Risk Tolerance:

Concept Definition Level
Risk Appetite Amount of risk enterprise is WILLING to take to achieve strategic objectives Enterprise level (strategic)
Risk Tolerance Acceptable DEVIATION from risk appetite; amount of risk enterprise can withstand Departmental/project level (tactical)

Key: Risk appetite is strategic; risk tolerance is tactical. Both are decided by senior leadership.

Risk Response Options

Memory trick: "AMST" (Avoid, Mitigate, Share/Transfer, Accept)

Response Description
Avoid Eliminate risk exposure by not pursuing certain activities
Mitigate Lessen probability or impact through controls
Share/Transfer Share impact across multiple enterprises (partnership) OR transfer to third party (insurance, contracts)
Accept Acknowledge risk; move forward; formal monitoring implemented

⚠️ Red Flag: Risk rejection (ignoring risk) is NOT acceptable — IS auditor should treat it as a red flag!

IT Risk Management Levels

Level Concern
Operational Effectiveness/efficiency of IT systems; bypass of controls; loss/unavailability of key resources
Project Ability to understand/manage project complexity; risk of not meeting objectives
Strategic IT capability alignment with business strategy; competitor comparison; technology change threats/opportunities

2.5.1 Developing a Risk Management Program

Steps: 1. Establish purpose — Align with enterprise strategy; define KPIs, KRIs, and metrics; senior management sets tone 2. Assign responsibility — Designate individual/team; integrate risk management at all levels

2.5.2 Risk Management Life Cycle

IDENTIFY → ASSESS → RESPOND/MITIGATE → MONITOR & REPORT → (cycle back)

Step 1: IT Risk Identification

Purpose: Identify information resources/assets needing protection because they are vulnerable to threats.

Threat Definition: Any circumstance or event that can cause harm to an information resource (destruction, disclosure, modification, denial of service).

Common IT Asset Categories: - Information and data - Hardware - Software - Documents - Personnel - Clients and customers

Step 2: IT Risk Assessment

Common Threat Classes: - Errors and omissions - Malicious damage/attack - Fraud - Theft - Equipment/software failure

Vulnerability: Characteristics of information resources that can be exploited by threats.

Vulnerability Examples: - Lack of user knowledge - Lack of security functionality - Inadequate user awareness (e.g., poor passwords) - Untested technology - Unprotected communications

Human Threat Actors: - Novices (script kiddies) - Hacktivists - Criminals - Terrorists - Nation-states - Civil unrest / political instability

Environmental Threats: - Floods, lightning, tornadoes, hurricanes, earthquakes, fires

Impact Types: - Direct loss of money - Breach of legislation (unauthorized disclosure) - Loss of reputation/goodwill - Endangerment of staff or customers - Breach of confidence - Loss of business opportunity - Reduction in operational efficiency - Interruption of business activity

Risk Formula:

Risk = Probability of Occurrence × Magnitude of Impact
Extended: Risk = Probability × Impact × Velocity

Residual Risk: Risk remaining AFTER controls are applied. - Risk ABOVE risk appetite → need stronger controls - Risk BELOW risk appetite → consider removing excessive controls (cost savings)

Final Residual Risk Acceptance Considers: - Organizational policy - Risk appetite - Risk identification and measurement - Uncertainty in risk assessment - Cost and effectiveness of controls - Cost vs. benefit analysis

Step 3: Risk Response and Mitigation

Countermeasures/Safeguards: Actions, devices, procedures, techniques (people, processes, products) to offset impact or probability of risk.

Control Strength Matrix:

STRONGEST → Preventive + Automated + Formal (stop occurrence, no human interaction needed) ↓ Preventive + Automated + Informal ↓ Preventive + Manual + Formal ↓ Detective → Corrective ↓
WEAKEST → Informal + Manual (deployed inconsistently)

Control Types: | Type | When It Acts | Description |------|-------------|-------------| Preventive | BEFORE event | Stops the risk from occurring | Detective | DURING event | Identifies when risk has occurred | Corrective | AFTER event | Repairs damage; recovers from event |

Hybrid Controls: Mix of manual and automated processes when full automation is not achievable.

Cost-Benefit Analysis: Controls selected based on: - Cost of control vs. benefit of minimizing risk - Management's risk appetite - Preferred risk-reduction methods

Step 4: Risk and Control Monitoring and Reporting

Purpose: Monitor performance levels; identify significant environment changes; trigger risk reassessment.

Three Sub-processes: Risk assessment + Risk mitigation + Risk reevaluation

Key Point: Risk assessment must be an ongoing process — continuously identify and evaluate risk as it arises.

2.5.3 Risk Analysis Methods

Method Description Pros Cons
Qualitative Words/descriptive rankings (High, Medium, Low) Simple, most frequently used Less rigorous; time-consuming analysis
Semiquantitative Descriptive rankings + numeric scale (e.g., High=5, Medium=3, Low=1) Reduces subjectivity of qualitative Still somewhat subjective
Quantitative Numeric/monetary values; historical data, statistical theories Measurable results Difficult to value information assets

Quantitative is used in BIA (Business Impact Analysis) — for financial enterprises under SOX

2.6 Data Privacy Program and Principles

Overview

Data Privacy Governance Program: Ensures all personal information within enterprise is identified and managed per legal requirements.

Personal Data Definition (general): Any piece of information that relates to an identifiable person.

Privacy Management Practice Areas:

  • Establishing privacy roles and responsibilities
  • Privacy training and awareness
  • Monitoring vendor and third-party management practices
  • Developing a privacy audit process
  • Implementing privacy incident management

Internal vs. External Privacy Requirements

Internal Requirements External Requirements
Assigning privacy roles Identifying applicable laws, regulations
Defining/inventorying personal information Choosing privacy principles and frameworks
Implementing employee privacy policies/procedures Posting a privacy notice
Establishing vendor management programs Maintaining third-party oversight
Managing privacy incidents Addressing data subject rights
Performing privacy audits Meeting customer/investor/industry requirements
Harmonizing with organizational culture Accommodating contractual relationships

Popular Privacy Frameworks/Principles:

  • ISO/TS 17975:2022 — Health Informatics
  • OECD Privacy Principles
  • AICPA Generally Accepted Privacy Principles
  • ISACA Privacy Principles

GDPR + DPF: EU's Trans-Atlantic Data Privacy Framework — protects EU individuals' data stored in USA.

2.6.1 Privacy Documentation

Types of Documentation

1. Privacy Notice: - Outward-facing statement written for data subjects and authorities - Describes how enterprise collects, uses, retains, safeguards, and discloses personal info - Establishes enterprise's legal accountability - Timing: Must allow sufficient time between notice and collection for data subjects to evaluate acceptance

Privacy Notice Purposes: - Explain what data is collected, why, how used, who it's shared with, how long retained, how destroyed - Inform data subjects of their rights - Support data subjects' consent - Build and maintain trust

Privacy Notice Formats: Web page, forms, brochures, documents at facilities, contracts, signs (CCTV warnings)

2. Consent Form: - Documents agreement of data subject with privacy notice terms - Provisions for: obtaining consent for existing data AND renewing consent if noncompliant - Formats: opt-in/opt-out button, tick box, signature box - Informed consent = ongoing exchange of information

Key: Data subjects have the right to withdraw consent at any time.

3. Personal Information Inventory: - Documented repository of personal data assets - Internally focused - Includes details about network, applications, systems, storage, physical forms - Must be kept updated

Other Privacy Documentation: - Activity logs - Data protection legal requirements - Privacy risk assessment reports - Privacy Impact Assessment (PIA) reports — determines if personal info is appropriately safeguarded - Privacy governance reports — communicate compliance levels to stakeholders - Training activity documentation - Data incident register — records of all personal data incidents - Individual rights register — records of requests from individuals about their records

2.6.2 Audit Process for Privacy

Privacy audits ensure: Enterprise's privacy policies, procedures, practices comply with internal/external requirements.

IS Auditors Evaluate: - Data privacy requirements in third-party contracts - Data retention and destruction policy implementation - Cross-border regulations addressed - Employee training on data privacy - Data inventory creation and maintenance - Data subject request handling

2.7 Data Governance and Classification

Data Governance Overview

First Step: Compile a detailed inventory of information assets.

Data Classification Benefits: - Enables cost-benefit analysis for appropriate level of control - Links data security to business objectives - Establishes consistent perspective on security requirements - Avoids overspending on unnecessary protection

Information Owner's Responsibility: Decide appropriate classification based on enterprise's data classification and handling policy.

Recommended Classification Levels: 3 to 5 levels (complex schemes become impractical)

Data Classification Scheme

Level Description Examples
Public Open and freely accessible; can be reused/redistributed Marketing material, press releases
Internal Strictly limited to employees with express access Business plans, org charts, reports
Confidential Must be kept private; negative impact if exposed Unpublished financial info, customer lists, PCI DSS/HIPAA data
Restricted Highly sensitive; criminal charges/legal fines if exposed Proprietary info, research data, regulated data

Data Classification Must Define: - Importance of information asset - Information asset owner - Process for granting access - Person responsible for approving access rights - Extent and depth of security controls

Data Classification Must Consider: Legal, regulatory, contractual, internal requirements for privacy, confidentiality, integrity, availability.

Important Rule: Application/system development programmers should NOT have access to production data or programs.

Key Risk of No Classification: Social engineering exploits users who don't know what information is confidential.

2.7.1 Data Inventory and Classification

PIA Requirement: A fully specified data inventory is the most important resource for conducting a Privacy Impact Assessment (PIA).

PIA = Privacy Impact Assessment: Process to determine if personal information is appropriately safeguarded, used, shared, made available to individuals, and destroyed.

Data Inventory Scope: Must be enterprise-wide and up-to-date.

2.7.2 Legal Purpose, Consent and Legitimate Interest

Three Legal Bases for Processing Personal Data:

Basis Definition
Legal Purpose Describe purpose in privacy notice; align subsequent use with stated purpose; comply with applicable law
Consent Obtain appropriate consent (implicit or explicit) before collection, before new use, before transfer to third parties
Legitimate Interest Legal basis for processing without consent when enterprise's interests are relevant (e.g., employee data for tax reports, COVID stats for public health)

Legitimate Interest Assessment Elements: 1. Purpose — What is the intended processing? 2. Necessity — Is the processing necessary? 3. Balance — Does individual's interest outweigh enterprise's interest?

2.7.3 Data Subject Rights

NIST Privacy Framework Functions: - Control-P — Develop activities to enable enterprises/individuals to manage data processing with sufficient granularity - Communicate-P — Develop activities to enable enterprises/individuals to have reliable understanding and engage in dialog about data processing

Transborder Data Flow: - Data transmitted between two countries - Legal compliance varies by country - Encryption requirements may differ - Internet routing may cross borders unknowingly

Part B: IT Management

2.8 IT Resource Management

Overview

IT Management: Oversees IT operations and resources; plans, builds, implements, operates, and monitors activities in alignment with governance direction.

Opportunity Cost: Investing in one IT project means foregoing other value-adding efforts.

IT Investment: Financial vs. Nonfinancial Benefits

Financial Benefits Nonfinancial Benefits
Cost reductions Improved operations
Revenue increases Strategic objective achievement
Budget impacts Improved customer satisfaction
Better information quality
Shorter cycle times

Best Practice: Transform nonfinancial benefits into monetary units where feasible.

2.8.1 Value of IT

IT Project Value = Benefits - Costs (larger benefit vs. cost = greater value)

IT Portfolio Management vs. IT Financial Management: - Portfolio management = explicitly directive, strategic (determines what to invest/divest) - Financial management = tracks and controls costs

2.8.2 IT Portfolio Management

Purpose: Determines if enterprise is pursuing the best IT-related projects to achieve enterprise goals.

Initial Implementation Steps: 1. Standardize terminology 2. Ensure management commitment 3. Plan portfolio management model 4. Specify portfolio inclusion criteria 5. Describe roles, tasks, decisions 6. Organize tools, support, instructions

Implementation Methods: - Analysis and assessment of risk profile - Diversification of projects, infrastructure, technologies - Alignment with business objectives (continuously) - Continuous improvement

Key Rule: Completed programs should NOT be deleted from portfolio — change status and evaluate results against original plans.

2.8.3 IT Management Practices

IT Management Activities: - Review policies/procedures impacting IT function - Assess HR management effectiveness - Review enterprise change management - Review financial management practices - Review information security management

2.8.4 Human Resource Management

HR Functions: Recruiting, hiring, training, promoting, measuring performance, disciplining, succession planning, retention, termination.

Recruiting and Hiring Controls:

  • Background checks (criminal, educational, financial, professional)
  • Confidentiality/NDA agreements
  • Employee bonding (protect against theft/mistakes — not accepted everywhere)
  • Conflict of interest disclosures
  • Codes of professional conduct/ethics
  • Noncompete agreements

Employee Handbook Contents:

Security policies, acceptable/unacceptable conduct, organizational values, company expectations, employee benefits, vacation policies, overtime rules, outside employment, performance evaluations, emergency procedures, disciplinary actions.

Training:

  • New-hire training — cybersecurity, confidentiality, data management, compliance, conflict of interest
  • Ongoing training — refresher + updated training for job functions and tools
  • Cross-training — multiple people trained for same job (backup, reduces key-person dependency)
  • Cross-training risk: Assess if one person knowing all parts of a system creates security exposure

Performance Management — SMART Goals:

  • Specific, clear, understandable
  • Measurable, verifiable, results-oriented
  • Attainable yet sufficiently challenging
  • Relevant to mission
  • Time-bound with schedule and milestones

Fraud Prevention Controls:

Control Purpose
Required Vacations / Mandatory Leave Forces someone else to perform job — may expose fraudulent activity
Job Rotation Reduces risk of fraudulent/malicious acts; guards against key-person dependency

⚠️ EXAM NOTE: Mandatory leave is a key control to detect/prevent internal fraud!

Termination Procedures:

  1. Return all devices, access keys, ID cards, badges
  2. Deletion/revocation of ALL login IDs and passwords (including app-specific credentials)
  3. Notification to appropriate staff and security personnel
  4. Arrangement of final pay routines
  5. Performance of termination interview

Important: Transfer/role changes also require access review similar to termination.

2.8.5 Enterprise Change Management

Definition: Defined and documented process to identify and apply technology improvements at infrastructure and application levels.

Process: 1. Senior management commitment obtained 2. IT works with functional areas to get support 3. Communication process to end users developed (explain changes, impact, expected benefits) 4. User feedback collected throughout project

User Involvement: Validation of business requirements, end-user acceptance testing, training.

2.8.6 Financial Management Practices

Cost Allocation (Chargebacks): - IT costs charged back to receiving departments - User-pays method improves IS expense monitoring - Policy established by board, jointly implemented by CFO, user management, IS management

IS Budget: Must be linked to short-term projects AND long-term IT objectives.

Software Capitalization: - Capital expense: on-premises purchased software - Operating expense: cloud-based subscription software - IS auditors help ensure teams track expenses properly against approved budget

2.8.7 Information Security Management

Primary Goal: Maintain CIA (Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability) of enterprise information.

Key Responsibilities: - Risk assessment and management - Security policy development - Incident response and management - Security awareness and training - Security architecture and design - Vulnerability management - BCP and DRP planning - Identity and access management (IAM) - Compliance and regulatory requirements - Security incident monitoring and analysis (SIEM tools)

Key Metrics: - RTO (Recovery Time Objective): Maximum acceptable downtime; when business must resume operations - RPO (Recovery Point Objective): Maximum acceptable data loss (expressed in time units)

2.9 IT Vendor Management

Overview

Why Vendor Management Matters: Increased reliance on third-party vendors = increased risk of security incidents, data breaches. Many significant data breaches originate through vendor access.

Best Practices for IT Vendor Risk Management: 1. Risk assessments on ALL potential vendors before awarding contracts 2. Include right-to-audit clauses in all vendor contracts 3. Principle of least privilege for vendor access 4. Maintain accurate inventory of all IT vendors and access levels 5. Remove vendor access when relationship ends 6. Monitor and certify all IT vendors regularly

2.9.1 Sourcing Practices

Sourcing Delivery Models:

Model Description
Insourced Fully performed by enterprise staff
Outsourced Fully performed by vendor staff
Hybrid Mix of enterprise and vendor staff; joint ventures

Location Models:

Location Description
Onsite Staff work at IT department location
Offsite/Nearshore Staff work remotely in same area
Offshore Staff at remote location in different geographic region

Outsourcing Decision Questions: - Is this a core function of the enterprise? - Can it be replicated externally at same or lower cost with equal or better quality? - Does the enterprise have experience managing third parties? - Are there regulatory restrictions on offshore or foreign nationals?

After Sourcing Decision — Steps: 1. Define IT function to be outsourced 2. Describe service levels required and minimum metrics 3. Know required knowledge, skills, quality of service provider 4. Know current in-house cost information 5. Conduct due diligence on potential providers 6. Confirm architectural considerations 7. Communicate decision to internal stakeholders

2.9.2 Outsourcing Practices and Strategies

Key Principle: While service delivery is TRANSFERRED to outsourcer, accountability remains firmly with management of the client enterprise.

Why Outsource: - Focus on core activities - Pressure on profit margins - Cost savings and faster time-to-market - Flexibility in organization, structure, market size

Outsourcing is both a STRATEGIC decision AND a procurement decision.

Outsourcing Advantages vs. Risks:

Advantages Risks
Economies of scale Costs exceeding expectations
Vendor expertise Loss of internal IT expertise
Better specifications from RFP Loss of control over IT
Reduced scope creep Vendor failure
Focus on core activities Deficient legal/regulatory compliance
Contract terms not met
Reputational damage
Loss/leakage of information

Risk Reduction Options: - Measurable, partnership-enacted shared goals and rewards - Software escrow - Multiple suppliers / withholding business as incentive - Periodic competitive reviews and benchmarking - Short-term contracts - Cross-functional contract management team - Contractual provisions for contingencies

Key Outsourcing Contract Provisions: - Service quality expectations (ISO, ITIL, CMMI methodologies) - Access control/security administration requirements - Violation reporting and follow-up with SLAs - Network control requirements - Specific performance parameters - Capacity management criteria - Dispute escalation and resolution process - Indemnification provisions - Confidentiality/NDA agreements - Right-to-audit provisions (access to facilities, records, copies, personnel) - Business continuity and disaster recovery provisions - CIA triad maintenance requirements - Intellectual property ownership - Software escrow provisions - Compliance with all relevant legal/regulatory requirements - Vendor must identify all subcontractors - Vendor must follow enterprise's policies (including ISP)

SLA (Service Level Agreement): - Stipulates and commits vendor to required level of service - Includes: guaranteed system uptime, customer support levels, HW/SW requirements - Includes: penalty provisions for services not provided - May include: bonuses/gain-sharing for exceeding service levels

2.9.3 Cloud Governance

Cloud Governance Challenges: - Business unit personnel can bypass IT to get cloud services directly - Policies must address sourcing, managing, and discontinuing cloud services

Cloud Risk Considerations: - Developing a cloud risk management strategy - Implementing strong cybersecurity controls - Managing cloud resilience, outsourcing, vendor lock-in, concentration risk - Ensuring staff have adequate skills for cloud risk management

KEY: The ultimate responsibility for information processed by an outsourcing party remains with the enterprise.

2.9.4 Governance in Outsourcing

Outsourcing Governance Objectives: - Ensure continuity of service at appropriate levels - Maintain profitability and added value for both parties

Governance Extensions for Both Parties: - Contractual viability through continuous review - Explicit governance schedule in contract - Manage via SLAs and OLAs (Operating Level Agreements) - Identify and manage all stakeholders - Clear roles for decision-making, escalation, dispute management - Continuous performance evaluation - Ongoing stakeholder communication

Business Alignment in Outsourcing: SLAs and OLAs must be established, monitored, and measured in terms of performance AND user satisfaction.

SOC Reports (Third-Party Audit Reports)

Framework: SSAE 18 (AICPA) replaces SAS 70; also ISAE 3402 (IAASB)

Report Focus
SOC 1 Internal controls relevant to user entity's financial reporting
SOC 2 Controls relevant to security, availability, processing integrity, confidentiality, or privacy
SOC 3 Similar to SOC 2 but does NOT include detailed design of controls or tests performed

IS Auditor Must Understand: - Management assertions - SSAE 18 reports (SOC 1, 2, 3) - Additional third-party reports (penetration tests, security assessments) - How to validate third-party assessments (independent, objective, competent auditors) - Internal processes for handling qualified opinions - Use of bridge letters (when new annual report isn't available)

2.9.5 Capacity and Growth Planning

  • Must reflect long- and short-range business plans
  • Considered within budgeting process
  • Changes in capacity reflect changes in infrastructure AND staff
  • Lack of qualified staff may lead enterprises to choose outsourcing for growth

2.10 IT Performance Monitoring and Reporting

Overview

Effective IT Performance Management Requires: - Monitoring process - KPIs (Key Performance Indicators) - KRIs (Key Risk Indicators) - KCIs (Key Control Indicators) - Systematic, timely performance reporting - Prompt action on deviations

2.10.1 Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)

Definition: A measure that determines how well the process is performing in enabling the goal to be reached.

KPI Characteristics: - Lead indicator of whether a goal will likely be reached - Good indicator of capabilities, practices, and skills - Often called "performance drivers" in Balanced Scorecards

Example KPI: Employee satisfaction with IT services — high rating indicates IT is meeting business needs.

KPI Criteria: Must measure relative success of changeover compared to desired performance objectives (e.g., percentage of errors, trouble reports, system outage duration, customer satisfaction).

2.10.2 Key Risk Indicators (KRIs)

Definition: Early warning indicators that help identify exposure to risk events that may harm business performance.

KRI Characteristics: - Usually include a threshold — when exceeded, alerts are sent - Indicate increased threat levels

Example KRI: Percentage increase in phishing attempts — signals increased threat level.

2.10.3 Key Control Indicators (KCIs)

Definition: Measures how well a control performs in reducing causes, consequences, or likelihood of risk.

Example KCI: Percentage of IT assets compliant with security policies.

Developing Metrics (4 Steps):

  1. Identify critical data points needed to achieve business objectives
  2. Identify specific, quantifiable outputs of work from identified processes
  3. Establish targets against which results can be scored
  4. Monitor performance against metrics and report to those who can adjust

Effective Metric Criteria: - Consistently measured - Based on acceptable best practices - Useful for internal and external comparison - Meaningful to IT's customers and sponsors - Accurately gathered - Expressed as a number, percentage, or unit of measure - Contextually specific

IS Auditor Ensures Performance Metrics Cover: - Long-term organizational objectives (technical, financial, operational) - Performance against short-term business and IT plans - Risk and compliance with regulations - Internal and external user satisfaction with service levels - Key IT processes (solution and service delivery) - Future-oriented activities (emerging technology, skills)

2.10.4 Performance Optimization

Definition: Improving efficiency of an information system while maintaining or increasing perceived service performance with minimal additional infrastructure investment.

Performance Governance is Achieved When: 1. Goals set by senior leadership aligned with approved business objectives 2. Metrics established by process owners aligned with goals 3. Performance monitored by each layer of management

Critical Success Factors (CSFs) for Performance Optimization: - Leadership support (senior leadership accountability) - Clear goals and objectives - Data-driven decision making - Employee involvement - Continuous improvement (ongoing process, not one-time)

2.10.5 Approaches and Techniques

PDCA Cycle (Plan-Do-Check-Act)

PLAN → DO → CHECK → ACT → (cycle repeats)
Step Description
Plan Establish objectives and processes; start small scale to test effects
Do Implement plan; collect data for analysis
Check Compare actual vs. expected results; chart data to see trends
Act Request corrective actions; analyze differences; apply changes

Six Sigma and Lean Six Sigma

  • Six Sigma: Measurement-oriented strategy for process improvement and defect reduction. Defect = anything outside customer specifications.
  • Lean Six Sigma: Same as Six Sigma but also eliminates unnecessary steps that don't add value.
  • Both are quantitative (data-driven) approaches.

Business Agility / Agile Methodology

Core Idea: Deliver value in smaller increments; adjust based on changing needs.

Key Agile Principles: 1. Customer collaboration 2. Iterative development (sprints) 3. Self-organizing teams 4. Continuous feedback 5. Adaptability and flexibility 6. Emphasis on simplicity

Popular Agile Frameworks: - Scrum — time-boxed sprints, product backlog, product owner, scrum master - Kanban — visual workflow management - Extreme Programming (XP) — engineering best practices

IT Balanced Scorecard (BSC)

Purpose: Management evaluation technique for assessing IT functions and processes beyond traditional financial evaluation.

Four Perspectives of IT BSC:

Perspective Description
User Orientation End user's perspective/evaluation of IT
Business Contribution Business value of IT investments
Operational Excellence IT processes used to develop and deliver IT resources
Future Orientation Human and technology resources needed for future delivery

BSC vs. Portfolio Management:

IT Balanced Scorecard IT Portfolio Management
Emphasizes vision and strategy Emphasizes agility in adjusting investments
Operationalizes strategic plan Built-in feedback mechanism for investments
Strategic planning feedback loop Considers entire program investments

Using BSC Measurement Framework (multilayer): - Mission → Strategies → Measures (KPIs, KRIs, KCIs) → Sources

Additional Performance Approaches

Approach Description
Benchmarking Systematically compares enterprise performance against peers/competitors
Business Process Reengineering (BPR) Thorough analysis and significant redesign of business processes for dramatic performance improvements
Root Cause Analysis Diagnosis to establish origins of events (root causes); develop controls to prevent recurrence
Life Cycle Cost-Benefit Analysis Assesses costs vs. outcomes for strategic IT direction; considers LCC (Life Cycle Cost) and BA (Benefit Analysis)

2.11 Quality Assurance and Quality Management of IT

Overview

QA Purpose: Provides confidence that products conform to established technical requirements and that personnel follow quality processes.

2.11.1 Quality Assurance (QA) vs. Quality Control (QC)

Aspect Quality Assurance (QA) Quality Control (QC)
Nature Process-focused Product/output-focused
Purpose Ensure quality processes are followed Verify product is free from defects
Type Proactive (prevention) Reactive (detection)
When Throughout development Before moving to production
Example Sets up procedures (ISO 9001-compliant) Tests/reviews software for defects

QA in the Enterprise: - Verifies system changes are authorized, tested, and implemented in a controlled manner - Oversees proper segregation of developer access to production - Maintains program versions and source code integrity

QA Independence Rules: - QA group should be independent within the enterprise - No individual should review their own work - Review should NOT be performed by someone with SoD conflict (e.g., DBA reviewing changes to database)

2.11.2 Quality Management

Definition: How IT department processes are controlled, measured, and improved.

Process Definition: Set of tasks that, when properly performed, produce desired results.

Quality Management Areas of Control: - Software development, maintenance, implementation - Hardware and software acquisition - Day-to-day operations - IT control performance - Service management - Security - HR management - General administration

Quality Standards: ISO 9001, CMMI, ITIL, ISO/IEC methodologies — assist in creating predictable, measurable, repeatable, and certified IT environments.

2.11.3 Operational Excellence

Operational Excellence Teams' Responsibilities: - Developing and implementing best practices - Providing training and support - Conducting research and development - Managing knowledge and information - Serving as a resource for employees and stakeholders

Goal: Help enterprises improve efficiency, reduce costs, improve customer satisfaction.

Case Study Key Lessons

Accenco Financial Institution — Key Findings

Issue Found CISA Lesson
Informal, incomplete IT strategy documents Greatest concern for IT-business strategy — IT loses sight of enterprise direction
Risk management committee met only 3 times, no minutes Indicates lack of good risk governance
No full-time CIO (CFO acts as CIO) Concern, but less critical than strategy issues
IT budgets don't match strategic plans Budget inadequacy is a concern but not the biggest
IS Manager reports to CFO Greatest IT governance concern — IS Manager should report to CEO or Board, not CFO
DBA approves application programmer access to production data Greatest SoD concern — data owners (business owners) should approve, not DBAs
Business managers sign off on financial results Best data integrity control — detects discrepancies from unauthorized data access

Case Study Key Answers (Exam-Ready)

  1. Greatest concern for IT-business strategy → A. Strategy documents are informal and incomplete (IT loses strategic direction)
  2. Most significant issue → B. Lack of IT policies and procedures (makes IT work inconsistently delivered)
  3. Greatest IT governance concern → D. IS manager reports to CFO (should report to CEO/Board for independence)
  4. Greatest SoD concern → A. DBA approves application programmer direct-write access (business owners, not DBAs, should approve)
  5. Best data integrity control → D. Business managers review and sign off on financial results (detects discrepancies from inappropriate data access)

Probable Exam Questions

High-Priority Exam Questions with Answers

Q1. For management to effectively monitor the compliance of processes and applications, which would be MOST ideal?

A. Dashboard — provides information illustrating compliance with processes, applications, and configurable elements

Q2. Which would be included in an IS strategic plan?

B. Analysis of future business objectives — IS strategic plans must address future business objectives (NOT hardware specs, project dates, or annual budgets)

Q3. Long-range planning for the IT department should recognize:

C. Enterprise goals, technological advances and regulatory requirements

Q4. What is the MOST important responsibility of a data security officer?

A. Recommending and monitoring data security policies (NOT establishing procedures — that's IT dept; NOT administering access — that's IT dept)

Q5. What is the MOST critical element for successfully implementing an information security program?

B. Senior management commitment (NOT ERM framework, NOT budget, NOT planning alone)

Q6. IT governance performance measures should:

A. Evaluate the activities of IT oversight committees (not evaluate IT dept; not adhere to regulatory reporting; not provide strategic drivers)

Q7. Which tasks may be performed by the SAME person in a well-controlled environment?

D. System development and system maintenance (both need dev access, not production access)

Q8. Most critical control over DBA?

B. Separation of duties regarding access rights — preventive control that prevents conflicting functions

Q9. In online systems where complete SoD is impossible, which function should be SEPARATED?

B. Authorization — must always be separated from origination, recording, and correction

Q10. In a small enterprise where SoD is impractical (operator + programmer), IS auditor should recommend:

C. Procedures that verify only approved program changes are implemented — compensating control (code comparisons reviewed by third party)

Q11. Which is the GREATEST concern from an IT governance perspective when IS Manager reports to CFO?

D. The IS manager reporting to the CFO — should report to CEO or Board for independence

Q12. Who approves application programmer access to production data?

Business owners/data owners — NOT the DBA (DBA is a custodian, not an owner)

Q13. What is the purpose of mandatory vacations?

To detect fraudulent activity by having another person perform the job — someone else may notice discrepancies

Q14. Risk remaining after controls have been applied is called:

Residual risk

Q15. Which type of risk analysis uses monetary values?

Quantitative analysis — uses numeric/monetary values; most rigorous; used in BIA

Q16. EGIT is primarily concerned with:

  1. IT delivers value to the business, AND 2. IT risk is managed

Q17. Who is accountable for information security governance globally?

Board of Directors and CEOs

Q18. What is the key difference between the IT Strategy Committee and IT Steering Committee?

Strategy Committee = advises board (strategic/future) | Steering Committee = assists executive (implementation/operational)

Q19. When an enterprise outsources services, accountability:

Remains with the client enterprise (service delivery is transferred, accountability is NOT)

Q20. SOC 2 reports cover:

Security, availability, processing integrity, confidentiality, or privacy controls at a service organization

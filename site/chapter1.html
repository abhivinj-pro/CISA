1. OVERVIEW

What is IS Audit?

IS Audit is the formal examination and/or testing of information systems to determine whether:

  • IS are in compliance with applicable laws, regulations, contracts and/or industry guidelines
  • IS and related processes comply with governance criteria, policies and procedures
  • CIA (Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability) of IS data meet appropriate levels
  • IS operations are being accomplished efficiently and effectiveness targets are being met

IS vs IT — Know the Difference!

Term Definition
Information System (IS) Combination of strategic, managerial and operational activities and related processes for gathering, processing, storing, distributing and using information + its related technologies (includes people + process + IT)
Information Technology (IT) The hardware, software, communication and other facilities used to input, store, process, transmit and output data

Simple Rule: IS = IT + People + Process. IT is just the technology component.

Three Major Audit Process Phases

[Planning] → [Fieldwork/Documentation] → [Reporting/Follow-Up]
  • Planning — Defines strategy, scope and objectives
  • Fieldwork/Documentation — Evidence gathering, testing controls, documenting results
  • Reporting/Follow-Up — Draft/issue report, follow up on corrective actions

Subphases can be customized as long as they comply with applicable standards (e.g., ITAF).

ISACA Code of Professional Ethics — 7 Principles

🔑 EXAM NOTE: You don't need to memorize numbers — understand and apply the principles.

  1. Support implementation and compliance with appropriate standards for IS governance and management
  2. Perform duties with objectivity, due diligence and professional care per professional standards
  3. Serve stakeholders lawfully; maintain high standards of conduct; don't discredit the profession
  4. Maintain privacy and confidentiality of information obtained — don't use for personal benefit or release to inappropriate parties
  5. Maintain competency — only undertake activities you can complete with required skills and knowledge
  6. Inform appropriate parties of results — disclose all significant facts known (don't distort results)
  7. Support professional education of stakeholders on IS governance and management

Memory Aid: SPSMIC → Support, Perform, Serve, Maintain (privacy), Maintain (competency), Inform, Create (education)

ITAF™ (IT Assurance Framework)

A comprehensive, best-practice reference model that: - Establishes standards for IS auditor roles, responsibilities, knowledge and skills - Defines terms and concepts specific to IS assurance - Provides guidance on planning, design, conduct and reporting of IS audit assignments

EXAM NOTE: You will NOT be tested on ITAF's organizational arrangement, but you WILL be tested on applying the standards.

IS Internal Audit Function

Audit Charter — Key Facts

  • Establishes the mandate to perform IS audit
  • Approved by the Board of Directors and Audit Committee (or senior management if these don't exist)
  • Covers the entire scope of audit activities for an entity
  • States management's responsibility, objectives and delegation of authority
  • Should only be changed if the change is thoroughly justified
  • Overarching document (vs. engagement letter which is specific to one audit)
Document Scope
Audit Charter Entire scope of all audit activities in an entity
Engagement Letter Specific to a particular audit exercise with specific objective
Contract/SOW For external IS audit firms

Reporting Line

  • IS audit function should be independent and report to:
  • The audit committee (if one exists), OR
  • The highest management level (e.g., board of directors)

Using Other Auditors/Experts

When outsourcing IS audit work, consider: - Laws and regulations restricting outsourcing - Audit charter or contractual stipulations - Impact on IS audit risk and professional liability - Independence and objectivity of external experts - Professional competence and qualifications

KEY POINT: Even if audit WORK is outsourced, professional liability is NOT necessarily transferred. The original IS auditor/entity remains responsible.

1.2 TYPES OF AUDITS, ASSESSMENTS & REVIEWS

Audit vs. Assessment vs. Review

  • Formal Audit → Highest level of assurance; formal inspection and verification
  • Assessment/Review → Less formal; less negative stigma; focus on opportunities for improvement

Types of Audits — Comprehensive Table

Audit Type Purpose Key Points
IS Audit Collect/evaluate evidence on IS safeguarding, integrity, availability, efficiency Broadest IS-specific audit
Compliance Audit Test controls for adherence to regulations or industry standards Often overlaps with other audits
Financial Audit Assess accuracy of financial reporting Involves substantive testing; IS auditors use risk/control-based approach
Operational Audit Evaluate internal control structure in a given process/area IS audits of application controls are examples
Integrated Audit Combines financial + operational audit steps May or may not use IS auditor; single comprehensive report
Administrative Audit Assess efficiency of operational productivity Focused on operations efficiency

Fraud Audit Discover fraudulent activity Uses specific tools and data analysis

1.2.1 Control Self-Assessment (CSA)

What is CSA?

An assessment of controls made by the staff and management of the unit being assessed — NOT the IS auditor. IS auditor acts as facilitator.

Key Objective

Leverage the internal audit function by shifting some control monitoring responsibilities to functional areas — NOT to replace audit, but to ENHANCE it.

CSA Implementation Methods

  • Questionnaires and surveys
  • Facilitated workshops (IS auditor leads, management participates)
  • Informal peer reviews

Benefits of CSA

Benefit Explanation
Early detection of risk Proactive risk identification
Better internal controls More effective and improved controls
Cohesive teams Employee involvement creates ownership
Sense of ownership Employees take control accountability
Increased awareness Better understanding of objectives and risk
Reduced control cost More efficient monitoring
Improved audit rating Better audit outcomes
Assurance to stakeholders Management demonstrates control adequacy

Disadvantages of CSA

  • Could be mistaken as a replacement for audit (it's NOT)
  • May be regarded as additional workload
  • Failure to act on suggestions can damage employee morale
  • Lack of audit knowledge may limit effectiveness in detecting weak controls

IS Auditor's Role in CSA

  • IS auditor is the FACILITATOR (not the evaluator)
  • Management is the PARTICIPANT
  • IS auditor guides auditees in assessing their environment based on risk

🔑 EXAM FAVORITE: "KEY benefit of CSA = Management ownership of internal controls is reinforced." This is the #1 tested answer!

1.2.2 Integrated Auditing

Definition

Combining appropriate audit disciplines (IS + financial + operational) to assess key internal controls over an operation, process or entity — focused on risk.

Integrated Audit Process Steps:

  1. Identify risk faced by the organization for the area being audited
  2. Identify relevant key controls
  3. Review and understand the design of key controls
  4. Test IT system support for key controls
  5. Test operational effectiveness of management controls
  6. Issue a combined report/opinion on control risk, design and weaknesses

Benefits of Integration:

  • Single audit with one comprehensive report
  • Staff development and retention (variety in work)
  • Process owners understand the linkage between controls and audit procedures
  • Management understands the linkage between control effectiveness and IT resource use
  • Shareholders understand corporate governance impact on financial statements

1.3 RISK-BASED AUDIT PLANNING

What is Audit Planning?

Establishing the overall audit strategy and detailing specific procedures at the beginning of the audit.

Planning Type Focus
Short-term Audit issues to be covered during the current year
Long-term Risk-related issues regarding changes in the organization's IT strategic direction

Audit Universe

The audit universe lists ALL processes that may be considered for audit. Each process undergoes a risk assessment.

Example — Reputation Risk Rating Scale:

Rating Criteria
High Reputational damage recovery takes >6 months
Medium Recovery takes 3–6 months
Low Recovery takes <3 months

Processes rated "high" form the ideal annual audit plan. Resource gaps are documented to show management the risk accepted.

Important: Annual planning analysis should occur at least annually and must be: - Reviewed by senior audit management - Approved by the audit committee or board of directors - Communicated to relevant management levels - Updated when key risk environment changes (e.g., acquisitions, new regulations)

Steps to Perform Audit Planning (Fig. 1.3)

1. Understand the organization's mission, objectives, purpose and processes
2. Understand governance structure and practices related to audit objectives
3. Understand changes in the business environment
4. Review prior work papers
5. Identify policies, standards, guidelines, procedures and organizational structure
6. Perform a risk analysis to design the audit plan
7. Set the audit scope and objectives
8. Develop the audit approach/strategy
9. Assign personnel resources
10. Address engagement logistics
11. Identify opportunities for continuous audit/automation using CAATs

1.3.1 Individual Audit Assignments

When planning individual audits, IS auditors must understand: - General business practices and functions relating to the audit subject - Types of information systems and technology supporting the activity - The regulatory environment in which the business operates

Consider: System implementation/upgrade deadlines, current/future technologies, requirements from business process owners, IS resource limitations.

1.3.2 Effect of Laws and Regulations on IS Audit Planning

Two major areas of concern: 1. Legal requirements applicable to audit or IS audit (laws, regulations, contracts) 2. Legal requirements placed on the auditee regarding its systems, data management, reporting

Steps to determine compliance with external requirements: 1. Identify government or other external requirements (electronic data, personal data, copyrights, IS practices, IS audits) 2. Document applicable laws and regulations 3. Assess whether management has considered external requirements in plans, policies, standards 4. Review internal IT documents addressing adherence to applicable laws 5. Determine adherence to established procedures 6. Determine if procedures ensure contracts with external IT providers reflect legal requirements

EXAM NOTE: CISA candidates will NOT be asked about specific laws/regulations but may be asked HOW to audit for compliance.

Risk-Based Audit Planning — Key Requirements

An IS auditor must understand: - External and internal factors affecting the organization - Selection and application of policies and procedures - Organization's objectives and strategies - Measurement and review of organization's performance

Also understand key components: - Strategy management - Business products and services - Corporate governance process - Transaction types, partners and flows within information systems

1.3.3 Audit Risk and Materiality

Audit Risk Definition

The risk that information collected may contain a material error that goes undetected during the audit.

Four Types of Audit Risk

Risk Type Definition Key Point
Inherent Risk Risk level/exposure WITHOUT considering controls management has implemented Exists independent of the audit; due to nature of business
Control Risk Risk of material error NOT prevented/detected on timely basis by internal controls High for manual log reviews; low for computerized data validation
Detection Risk Risk that material errors/misstatements will NOT be detected by the IS auditor Reduced by proper sampling and quality control
Overall Audit Risk Risk that auditor may NOT detect a material error in financial/information reports Objective: limit to a sufficiently low level

Materiality

  • Refers to the importance/significance of information regarding its impact on the entity being audited
  • Inverse relationship: Higher materiality → Lower acceptable audit risk (and vice versa)
  • An individual error may be minor, but combined with others could become material

🔑 KEY DISTINCTION: Audit risk ≠ Statistical sampling risk. Sampling risk = risk that incorrect assumptions are made about characteristics of a population from which a sample is drawn.

1.3.4 Risk Assessment

Who is Responsible?

  • IT management is responsible for conducting risk assessments
  • IS auditor may ASSIST if expertise is not present internally
  • IS auditor may perform a separate risk assessment for audit planning purposes

Risk assessments should: - Identify, quantify and prioritize risk - Guide management action and priorities for implementing controls - Be performed periodically and whenever significant changes occur

1.3.5 IS Audit Risk Assessment Techniques

Technique Description
Simple Classification High/Medium/Low based on auditor's judgment
Scoring System Variables (technical complexity, financial loss, control procedures) assigned weights
Subjective Assessment Based on business knowledge, management directives, historical perspectives

IS auditors should leverage management's risk assessment results (with professional skepticism due to potential independence impairment).

Benefits of Risk Assessment-Based Auditing:

  • Enables effective allocation of limited audit resources
  • Ensures relevant information from all management levels
  • Establishes basis for managing the audit department
  • Provides summary of how audit subjects relate to overall organization and business plans

1.3.6 Risk Analysis

  • A subset of risk assessment used during audit planning
  • Identifies risk and vulnerabilities so an IS auditor can determine controls needed
  • Does NOT provide sufficient evidence to support the audit opinion (only helps plan)

1.4 TYPES OF CONTROLS AND CONSIDERATIONS

What is a Control?

A control prevents, detects and/or contains an incident and enables recovery from a risk event.

Controls are composed of policies, procedures, practices and organizational structures implemented to reduce risk.

1.4.1 Internal Controls

  • Operate at ALL levels within an organization
  • Board of directors and senior management are responsible for establishing appropriate culture
  • Two key aspects of controls: 1. What should be achieved 2. What should be avoided

Control activities include: approvals, authorizations, verifications, reconciliations, performance reviews, asset security, separation of duties (SoD).

1.4.2 Control Objectives and Control Measures

Term Definition
Control Objective Desired result/purpose to be achieved by implementing control activities; linked to company's strategic goals
Control Measure Activity contributing to fulfillment of a control objective

Control Objectives relate to:

  • Effectiveness and efficiency of operations
  • Reliability of financial reporting
  • Compliance with applicable laws and regulations
  • Safeguarding information assets

Specific IS Control Objectives include:

  • Safeguarding information assets from improper access
  • Ensuring SDLC processes are established and effective
  • Ensuring integrity of OS environments and network management
  • Ensuring integrity of sensitive application environments through:
  • Authorization of input (each transaction authorized and entered once)
  • Validation of input (validated, no negative impact on processing)
  • Accuracy and completeness of transaction processing
  • Reliability of overall information processing
  • Accuracy, completeness and security of output
  • Database CIA (confidentiality, integrity, availability)
  • Appropriate identification and authentication of users
  • Efficiency and effectiveness of operations
  • Compliance with laws and regulations
  • Availability of IT services (BCPs/DRPs)
  • Protection via incident response plans
  • Integrity via change management procedures
  • Outsourced processes with clearly defined SLAs

General Control Methods (Fig. 1.5)

Category Description Examples
Managerial (Administrative) Controls related to oversight, reporting, procedures and operations Policies/procedures, accounting controls, employee training, compliance reporting
Technical (Logical) Controls provided through use of technology/equipment (requires proper managerial controls to work) Firewalls, IDS, passwords, antimalware
Physical Controls that physically restrict access to facility or hardware (require maintenance and monitoring) Access badges, locks, CCTV

Business Application Controls

Business System Risk IS-Specific Control
Ecommerce SQL injection attacks Secure coding training, SDLC code reviews, input validity checks
EDI Data interception/manipulation Encryption controls
Email Social engineering/phishing Spam filtering, hyperlink verification, phishing training
ICS/SCADA Direct impact on human life if compromised Perimeter security, network segmentation, MFA
AI/Expert Systems Inherent bias in decision trees Ensure proper expertise used in developing assumptions/formulas

1.4.3 Control Classifications

Control Type Purpose Examples
Preventive Inhibit/impede attempts to violate security (strongest) Encryption, user authentication, vault doors, MFA
Deterrent Discourage intentional/unintentional compromise Warning banners, acceptable use policies, security cameras
Detective Warn of violations WITHOUT stopping them Audit trails, IDS, checksums
Corrective Remediate errors, omissions, unauthorized uses when detected Data backups, error correction, automated failover
Compensating Offset deficiency when baseline controls cannot meet requirements due to constraints Isolated network segments, third-party challenge-response

Control Purpose Visual Framework:

REDUCE LIKELIHOOD MITIGATE CONSEQUENCES Preventive controls + Corrective controls Detective controls + Recovery procedures

🔑 KEY EXAM POINT: - Preventive > Detective in strength (preventive stops the threat) - Corrective = helps recover from impact - Backup and restore = CORRECTIVE (not preventive, not detective)

Control Interaction with Threats (Fig. 1.9):

Threat → Deterrent Control → Preventive Control → [if bypassed] → Detective Control ↓ Compensating Control ↓ Corrective Control → Reduces Impact

1.4.4 Control Relationship to Risk

  • Direct relationship: Risk is addressed through control; control is justified by the risk it addresses
  • If controls don't mitigate risk to acceptable level → additional controls needed
  • If appropriate countermeasures can't be implemented → use compensating controls
  • Compensating controls must achieve the same result as the underperforming control

1.4.5 Prescriptive Controls and Frameworks

Organizations may use prescriptive control sets as a reference:

Framework Focus
CIS 18 Critical Security Controls Prioritized cybersecurity best practices
OWASP SAMM Software security strategies
SOC Reports Service organization control assurance
PCI DSS Credit card data security requirements
CSA Cloud Controls Matrix (CCM) Cloud security controls

When prescriptive controls don't apply to an organization, reasons and validation of non-applicability must be formally documented.

1.4.6 Evaluation of the Control Environment

Management Control Monitoring

Management performs its own monitoring to: - Ensure control requirements are met - Ensure standards are being followed - Ensure employees comply with policies

IS auditor may leverage management's monitoring results as reassurance (not a substitute for independent audit).

IS auditor should ensure management monitoring processes include: - Remediation of identified exceptions and lessons learned - Metrics established for critical processes based on risk assessment - Specific, quantifiable outputs for reporting - Independence considerations - Reporting that establishes thresholds and tracks success over time

1.5 AUDIT PROJECT MANAGEMENT

Overview

Once the audit is planned, the IS auditor executes it through these key steps:

1. Plan the audit engagement (project-specific risk)
2. Build the audit plan (chart tasks across a timeline, estimate time)
3. Execute the plan
4. Monitor project activity (actual vs. planned progress)

1.5.1 Audit Objectives

  • Audit objective = specific goals that must be accomplished by the audit
  • Control objective = how an internal control SHOULD function (different concept!)
  • IS auditor must translate broad audit objectives into specific IS audit objectives

Primary purposes of IS audit:

  • Identify control objectives and related controls
  • Determine whether to perform compliance or substantive testing
  • Identify both general and application controls

1.5.2 Audit Phases — Detailed Breakdown

Planning Phase (Fig. 1.12)

Audit Step Description
1. Determine audit subject Identify area to be audited (business function, system, location)
2. Define audit objective Identify purpose (e.g., "determine if program changes occur in a controlled environment")
3. Set audit scope Specific systems, function or unit to include; define testing points and technical skills needed
4. Perform preaudit planning Risk assessment; interview auditee; identify regulatory requirements; define resources, budget, timeline, communication plan
5. Determine procedures and data gathering steps Identify policies/standards; regulatory requirements; interview list; tools; test scripts; evaluation criteria

Fieldwork/Documentation Phase (Fig. 1.13)

Audit Step Description
1. Acquire data Establish process to collect audit evidence; advance request list; secure collection (e.g., fileshare); GRC tools
2. Test controls Use interviews, observations, inspections; sampling when needed
3. Discover and validate issues Identify deviations from expected outcomes (policy requirements)
4. Document results Document in audit program and work papers per organizational standards

Reporting/Follow-Up Phase (Fig. 1.14)

Audit Step Description
1. Gather report requirements Identify reporting requirements from internal standards or external requirements
2. Draft report Reviewed by IS audit leadership before auditee review; includes overall results and recommendations
3. Issue report Finalized and issued; retained per retention requirements; presented to oversight function (audit committee)
4. Follow up Establish process to follow up on management's remediation progress

1.5.3 Audit Programs

An audit program is a step-by-step set of audit procedures and instructions to complete an audit.

Main Purposes:

  • Formal documentation of audit procedures and sequential steps
  • Creation of repeatable, easy-to-use procedures
  • Documentation of testing type (compliance and/or substantive)
  • Meeting generally accepted audit standards

General Audit Procedures:

  1. Obtain and record understanding of audit area/subject
  2. Create risk assessment and general audit plan/schedule
  3. Perform detailed audit planning (steps, timeline)
  4. Conduct preliminary review
  5. Evaluate the audit area/subject
  6. Verify and evaluate appropriateness of controls
  7. Conduct compliance testing (tests of controls)
  8. Conduct substantive testing (confirms accuracy of information)
  9. Report (communicate results)
  10. Follow up (internal audit function)

Minimum Skills for Developing an Audit Program:

  • Understanding of enterprise and industry to categorize risk
  • Knowledge of IT space and technologies
  • Understanding of relationship between business risk and IT risk
  • Knowledge of risk assessment practices
  • Understanding of testing procedures (GAS, specialized software, flowcharting, audit logs, inquiry, observations, walk-throughs, reperformance)

1.5.4 Audit Work Papers

  • Purpose: Bridge between audit objectives and final report
  • Work papers = all plans, programs, activities, tests, findings, incidents
  • Provide seamless transition with traceability and support from objectives to report
  • Should consider security — same controls assessed in the audit should protect work papers
  • Require a retention and destruction process based on legal requirements

1.5.5 Fraud, Irregularities and Illegal Acts

  • Management is primarily responsible for establishing controls to deter/detect fraud
  • IS auditors must be aware of fraud indicators and be alert to fraud possibilities
  • During regular assurance work, if indicators of fraud found → communicate need for investigation to appropriate authorities
  • Major fraud → communicate to audit committee in a timely manner
  • IS auditors should be aware of legal requirements for fraud reporting

1.5.6 Agile Auditing

Overview

Agile concepts applied to IS audit to provide faster and more efficient audit outcomes.

Agile vs. Traditional Audit

Dimension Traditional Audit Agile Audit
Planning Several months Weeks or even days (sprint cycles)
Phases Strictly separated Blurred/combined (run in parallel)
Scope Fixed Flexible (real-time adjustments)
Findings Reported at end Real-time assurance to customers
Plan review Annually Quarterly or more frequently

Benefits of Agile Auditing:

  • Reduced end-to-end planning (condensed sprint cycles)
  • Streamlined engagements (planning, fieldwork, reporting combined)
  • Direct customer collaboration (auditees in daily scrum/standup)
  • Flexible audit scope (adjustments as new information is discovered)
  • Real-time assurance (findings shared as discovered, not just at report end)
  • Frequent audit plan updates (quarterly review of audit backlog)

KEY: Agile does NOT compromise auditor objectivity, independence or professional skepticism.

1.6 AUDIT TESTING AND SAMPLING METHODOLOGY

Compliance vs. Substantive Testing

| Compliance Testing (Tests of Controls) | Substantive Testing (Tests of Details) |--|---------------------------------------|----------------------------------------| Purpose | Evaluate OPERATING EFFECTIVENESS of controls in preventing/detecting/correcting material weaknesses | Detect material weaknesses at the ASSERTION level; confirm accuracy of actual data | Question answered | "Are controls working?" | "Is the data accurate?" | Examples | User access rights, change control procedures, log reviews, license audits | Interest calculation on account sample, vouching transactions to supporting documents | When to use | To test if management policies are being followed | When controls are weak or need to verify data accuracy |

KEY RELATIONSHIP: Strong controls (good compliance test results) → Less substantive testing needed. Weak controls → More substantive testing required.

1.6.1 Compliance vs. Substantive Testing — Deeper Dive

[Preliminary Review] → [Assess control environment] ↓
[Compliance Testing] → Tests if controls are being applied ↓ (if controls strong) ↓ (if controls weak)
[Minimal Substantive] [Extensive Substantive Testing]

1.6.2 Sampling

Definition

Used when time and cost preclude testing ALL transactions. Sample = subset of population used for testing.

Two General Approaches

Approach Description
Statistical Sampling Objective method; uses mathematical laws of probability to calculate sample size, select items and evaluate results. Results are mathematically quantifiable.
Nonstatistical (Judgmental) Uses audit judgment for sample size and selection; decisions based on which items are most material and risky

Statistical Sampling Terminology

Term Definition
Confidence Coefficient/Level % probability that sample represents the population (e.g., 95% = high assurance). Higher confidence → larger sample.
Level of Risk 1 minus confidence coefficient (e.g., if 95% confidence → 5% risk)
Precision Acceptable range difference between sample and actual population. Higher precision → smaller sample. Lower precision → larger sample.
Expected Error Rate Estimated % of errors that may exist. Higher error rate → larger sample.
Sample Mean Average value of the sample
Sample Standard Deviation Variance of sample values from the mean (spread/dispersion)
Tolerable Error Rate Maximum misstatement/errors without material misstatement. Used as upper precision limit in compliance testing.
Population Standard Deviation Measures relationship to normal distribution; larger std deviation → larger sample

Types of Attribute Sampling (Compliance Testing — "How many?")

Type Purpose When to Use
Attribute Sampling (Fixed-size) Estimate rate (%) of occurrence of an attribute Standard compliance testing
Stop-or-Go Sampling Prevent excessive sampling; stop at earliest possible moment When you expect relatively few errors
Discovery Sampling Discover fraud, circumvention, irregularities When seeking out a specific irregularity; single error = entire population flagged

Types of Variable Sampling (Substantive Testing — monetary/quantity values)

Type Description
Stratified Mean Per Unit Population divided into groups; samples drawn from each group; smaller overall sample size
Unstratified Mean Per Unit Sample mean calculated and projected as estimated total
Difference Estimation Estimates total difference between audited and book (unaudited) values based on sample differences

Sampling Risk

Risk Description
Risk of Incorrect Acceptance Material weakness assessed as UNLIKELY when population IS materially misstated (dangerous — miss a problem!)
Risk of Incorrect Rejection Material weakness assessed as LIKELY when population is NOT materially misstated (wasteful — reject good data)

Steps in Sample Selection (Fig. 1.18):

1. Determine the objective
2. Define the population
3. Determine the method
4. [Execute and evaluate]

1.7 AUDIT EVIDENCE COLLECTION TECHNIQUES

What is Audit Evidence?

Any information used by an IS auditor to determine whether the entity or data being audited follows established criteria and supports audit conclusions.

Evidence must be: Sufficient, Relevant, and Competent

  • Competent = Both reliable AND relevant
  • Sufficient = Enough quantity (judgment-based)
  • Appropriate = High quality (the IFAC standard)

Evidence Reliability Determinants

Factor More Reliable Less Reliable
Source External sources Internal sources
Provider qualifications Highly qualified, relevant expertise Unknown/limited qualifications
Objectivity Objective, direct evidence Requires judgment or interpretation
Timing Current, available May not be retrievable later (e.g., dynamic spreadsheets)

Evidence-Gathering Techniques

Technique Description
Reviewing IS Organization Structures Evaluate SoD; assess organizational controls including distributed processing
Reviewing IS Policies and Procedures Verify appropriate policies exist; personnel understand them; they are being followed
Reviewing IS Standards Understand existing standards in the organization
Reviewing IS Documentation Review systems docs (feasibility studies, SLAs, test plans, security plans, BCPs, QA reports)
Interviewing Appropriate Personnel Organized, objective-driven; documented via notes; never accusatory
Observing Processes Unobtrusive; document in detail; can verify actual functions, procedures, reporting relationships
Reperformance Strongest evidence technique; actually perform the control in real time
Walk-throughs Confirm understanding of controls; identify evidence needed for effectiveness testing

🔑 Reperformance provides the BEST evidence when inquiry, observation and inspection are insufficient.

1.7.1 Interviewing and Observing Personnel

Interviewing Guidelines:

  • Organized in advance with clearly communicated objectives
  • Follow a fixed outline
  • Documented by interview notes
  • Use an interview form or checklist
  • Never accusatory — help people feel comfortable
  • Verify notes with the interviewee

Observation Identifies:

  • Actual functions — who is actually doing the job
  • Actual processes/procedures — walk-through for compliance evidence
  • Security awareness — understanding of preventive/detective security measures
  • Reporting relationships — SoD compliance

Observation Drawback:

Personnel, upon noticing they are being observed, may change their usual behavior — called the Hawthorne Effect.

1.8 AUDIT DATA ANALYTICS

What Can Data Analytics Do for IS Auditors?

  • Determine operational effectiveness of the current control environment
  • Determine effectiveness of antifraud procedures and controls
  • Identify business process errors and inefficiencies
  • Identify exceptions or unusual business rules
  • Identify fraud
  • Identify poor data quality areas
  • Conduct risk assessment at the planning phase

Data Analytics Process:

1. Set the scope (audit objectives, data needs, sources, reliability)
2. Identify and obtain the data
3. Validate the data: • Validate balances independent of data set • Reconcile detailed data to report control totals • Validate numeric, character and date fields • Verify time period of data set • Verify all necessary fields are in the acquired data set
4. Execute the tests (scripts, analytical tests)
5. Document the results (purpose, data sources, conclusions)
6. Review the results (adequate procedures, reviewed by qualified person)
7. Retain the results (program files, scripts, macros, data files)

Analytics Use Cases in Auditing:

  • Combine logical access files with HR employee master files for authorized users
  • Combine file library settings with change management data
  • Match ingress/egress records to identify tailgating in physical security logs
  • Review table/system configuration settings
  • Review system logs for unauthorized access
  • Test system conversion
  • Test logical access SoD (e.g., Active Directory + job descriptions)

1.8.1 Computer-Assisted Audit Techniques (CAATs)

What are CAATs?

Software tools that enable IS auditors to gather and analyze data independently during an IS audit, especially when different hardware/software environments make manual collection impossible.

Types of CAATs:

Tool Description
GAS (Generalized Audit Software) Reads data from various database platforms, flat files, ASCII formats. Supports: file access, file reorganization, data selection, statistical functions, arithmetic functions
Utility Software Subset of DBMS software (e.g., report generators) — provides evidence on control effectiveness
Test Data Sample data to assess logic errors and whether program meets objectives
Application Software Tracing & Mapping Traces application processing paths
Expert Systems Provides audit direction based on knowledge base of senior auditors
Debugging and Scanning Software Identifies vulnerabilities and weaknesses

CAATs Can Perform:

  • Tests of details of transactions and balances
  • Analytical review procedures
  • Compliance tests of IS general controls
  • Compliance tests of IS application controls
  • Network and OS vulnerability assessments
  • Penetration testing
  • Application security testing and source code security scans

CAATs Cost-Benefit Considerations:

  • Ease of use for audit staff; training requirements
  • Complexity of coding and maintenance
  • Flexibility of uses; installation requirements
  • Processing efficiencies
  • Ensuring data integrity (authenticity, timestamps)
  • Permission to install on auditee servers
  • Confidentiality of data being processed

CAATs Best Practice:

IS auditors should request read-only access to production data. Data manipulation must be done on copies in a controlled environment (NOT on production data).

1.8.2 Continuous Auditing and Monitoring

Concept Definition
Continuous Auditing IS auditor performs tests and assessments in real-time or near-real-time; reports results within much shorter time frame than traditional audit
Continuous Monitoring Organization observes performance of processes, systems or data (e.g., antivirus, IDS)
Continuous Assurance Achieved when BOTH continuous auditing AND monitoring take place

KEY: Continuous monitoring should NEVER be considered a substitute for the audit function (lacks independence and objectivity).

Continuous Auditing Advantages:

  • Captures internal control problems as they occur, preventing negative effects
  • Reduces delays, planning time, audit inefficiencies
  • Provides real-time data and exception reporting

Continuous Auditing Disadvantages:

  • Requires full top management support and extensive technical knowledge
  • Auditors may hesitate to trust automated tools over personal judgment
  • Need mechanisms to eliminate false negatives and false positives

1.8.3 Five Continuous Auditing Techniques

Technique Complexity When to Use
SCARF/EAM (System Control Audit Review File/Embedded Audit Modules) Very High Regular processing cannot be interrupted; audit software embedded in host applications
Snapshots Medium An audit trail is required; "pictures" of processing path for tagged transactions
Audit Hooks Low Only select transactions/processes need to be examined; embedded hooks/triggers
ITF (Integrated Test Facility) High Not beneficial to use test data; dummy entities in production files for testing
CIS (Continuous and Intermittent Simulation) Medium Transactions meeting certain criteria need to be examined; simulation during process run

Continuous Auditing Techniques Used at All Data Levels:

  • Transaction logging, query tools, statistics and data analysis, DBMSs
  • Data warehouses, data marts, data mining, intelligent agents
  • EAM, neural network technologies, XBRL standards

1.8.4 Artificial Intelligence in IS Audit

AI/ML Use Cases in IS Audit:

  • Detect fraudulent transactions
  • Perform data quality checks
  • Screen for negative news
  • Automate data processing

AI/ML Efficiency Gains:

  • Efficiency: Automating tedious manual processes (work paper markups, data manipulation)
  • Reduced risk: Ability to increase audit sample sizes; more time for analysis

AI/ML Techniques for IS Audit (Fig. 1.21):

Technique Application
Document Classification Understanding SOPs, policies; making inferences from previous audit reports
Text Summarization Generating audit observations; auto-generating checklists
Topic Analysis Analyzing data; building keyword rule engines
Search and Retrieval Making similar audit report inferences
Statistical Analysis Aggregating data; interpreting data
Sentiment Analysis Identifying key issues and risk; making intelligent inferences for reports

AI/ML Audit Risk and Considerations:

CRITICAL EXAM POINTS: - AI/ML results must ALWAYS be interpreted by a person - Training data must be correct and adequate (cover both usual and unusual cases) - Tendency to trust the machine must be validated by exhaustive testing - AI tools introduce ethics and bias of human judgment (stereotyping) - AI/ML are an evolution of CAATs — same considerations apply

1.9 REPORTING AND COMMUNICATION TECHNIQUES

Key Principles of Audit Reporting

  • Communication skills (written AND verbal) determine effectiveness of the reporting process
  • IS auditor needs: facilitation, negotiation and conflict resolution skills
  • Understand materiality (relative importance based on business impact)

1.9.1 Communicating Audit Results

Exit Interview (Conducted at End of Audit):

  • Discuss findings and recommendations with auditee management
  • Ensure facts are correct and material
  • Ensure recommendations are realistic and cost-effective
  • If not cost-effective → negotiate alternatives with auditee management
  • Recommend implementation dates for agreed-on recommendations

Escalation Path:

Discuss with auditee management first ↓ (if agreed)
Brief senior auditee management ↓
Present periodic summary to audit committee

IS auditors are ultimately responsible to senior management and the audit committee — attempts by lower levels to deny access would limit audit independence.

1.9.2 Audit Report Objectives (Six Objectives)

  1. Formally present audit results to the auditee (and audit client, if different)
  2. Serve as formal closure of the audit engagement
  3. Provide statements of assurance and identify areas needing corrective action
  4. Serve as valued reference for parties researching the auditee or audit topic
  5. Serve as basis for a follow-up audit if findings were presented
  6. Promote audit credibility (well-developed and well-written report)

1.9.3 Audit Report Structure and Contents

Audit reports usually include:

Section Content
Introduction Audit objectives, limitations, scope, period of coverage, audit procedures overview, methodology and guidelines
Audit Findings Presented in separate sections, often grouped by materiality and/or intended recipient
Overall Conclusion/Opinion Adequacy of controls and procedures examined; actual potential risk identified
Reservations/Qualifications Any limitations on the scope or conclusions
Detailed Findings & Recommendations Based on materiality and intended recipient (e.g., audit committee reports may exclude findings only relevant to local management)

Key Reporting Principles:

  • Provide a balanced report (positive constructive comments + negative findings)
  • IS auditor makes final decision on what to include or exclude
  • Report should be issued in a timely manner to encourage prompt corrective action
  • Prior communication of significant findings should NOT alter intent or content of final report
  • Minor findings may be communicated via memorandum rather than full report

1.9.4 Audit Documentation

What Must It Include? (Minimum)

  • Planning and preparation of audit scope and objectives
  • Description and/or walk-throughs of scoped audit area
  • Audit program
  • Audit steps performed and evidence gathered
  • Use of services of other auditors and experts
  • Audit findings, conclusions and recommendations
  • Document identification and dates

Also Recommended:

  • Copy of the report issued
  • Evidence of supervisory review

Documentation Standards:

  • Should be dated, initialed, page-numbered, relevant, complete, clear, self-contained, properly labeled
  • May be automated
  • Audit documentation is generally property of the auditee — access only to authorized personnel
  • For external parties requesting access → prior approval of senior management and legal counsel required

1.9.5 Follow-Up Activities

  • IS audit is an ongoing process — not effective without follow-up
  • IS auditors should have a follow-up program to check if corrective actions were implemented
  • Timing depends on criticality of findings and IS auditor's judgment
  • Results of follow-up communicated to appropriate levels of management

1.9.6 Types of IS Audit Reports

  • Most IS audits result in a single IS audit report
  • In some cases, multiple reports (e.g., + confidential security report with technical details)
  • Format depends on: scope, objectives, degree of IT process examination, requirements from audit client and auditee

Types of Engagement Reporting:

Engagement Type Description Assurance Level
Review Less comprehensive Lower assurance
Examination More comprehensive; requires stronger evidence for opinion Higher assurance
Agreed-Upon Procedures Narrowly defined scope; may include testing; no opinion expressed Limited/no opinion

1.10 QUALITY ASSURANCE AND IMPROVEMENT OF THE AUDIT PROCESS

1.10.1 Audit Committee Oversight

  • Audit committee is responsible for oversight of the IS audit function and interaction with the chief audit executive
  • If no audit committee exists → designated group or individual assumes oversight responsibilities
  • Continuous performance monitoring of the audit function should be established with periodic reporting

1.10.2 Audit Quality Assurance

  • Quality of individual audits is the responsibility of audit leadership and assigned project leads
  • They ensure documented audit procedures are followed
  • Documented procedures can be in audit manuals, wikis, sampling guidance, etc. — must be known to all IS audit team members
  • IS audit leadership reviews work papers and final deliverables (audit reports)
  • A formal review process should be established for all audit types based on risk and authoritative guidance

1.10.3 Audit Team Training and Development

  • A formal development plan should be established for all IS audit team members
  • Should include applicable training programs and certifications by role
  • IS audit leadership must ensure budget supports training and development needs

1.10.4 Monitoring

Monitoring Initiative Description
Audit QA Results of QA procedures periodically reviewed and summarized; identify trends and lessons learned; actionable items remediated and tracked formally
Independence Monitoring Process for IS auditors to self-report potential independence impairments; periodic checks; integrated into greater audit function
Certification and Accreditations Ownership assigned to IS audit leadership; ensure compliance with certification/accreditation bodies
Continued Professional Education Monitor compliance with CPE requirements from internal development plans or external certifying bodies

PROBABLE EXAM QUESTIONS

High-Probability Questions

1. Which of the following outlines the overall authority to perform an IS audit?

Answer: C. The approved audit charter (states responsibility, authority and accountability)

2. What is the KEY benefit of a Control Self-Assessment (CSA)?

Answer: A. Management ownership of internal controls supporting business objectives is reinforced

3. When developing a risk-based audit program, an IS auditor MOST likely focuses on:

Answer: A. Business processes (risk-based approach requires understanding business processes)

4. Which type of audit risk assumes an absence of compensating controls?

Answer: C. Inherent risk (assessed without considering any management controls)

5. The MOST critical step when planning an IS audit is:

Answer: D. Performance of a risk assessment (required by ISACA Standard 1201)

6. The approach for planning IS audit coverage should be based on:

Answer: A. Risk (risk-based approach is the foundation of audit planning)

7. The MOST important reason for reviewing audit planning at periodic intervals is:

Answer: B. To consider changes to the risk environment

8. A daily backup stored offsite and used to restore files is an example of a:

Answer: C. Corrective control (corrects/minimizes impact of a disruption)

9. An IS auditor finds a system software weakness during an application controls review. The IS auditor should:

Answer: D. Review the relevant system software controls and recommend a detailed system software review

10. Who is primarily responsible for conducting risk assessments?

Answer: IT management (IS auditor may assist but management is ultimately responsible)

11. What is the PRIMARY role of an IS auditor in a CSA workshop?

Answer: Facilitator (management is the participant, not the auditor)

12. Professional liability when IS audit is outsourced:

Answer: Liability is NOT necessarily transferred — the original auditor/entity remains responsible

13. In the integrated audit approach, the IS auditor's role during risk assessment is to:

Answer: Understand and identify risk under topical areas such as IT infrastructure, IT governance, and IT operations

14. The BEST evidence-gathering technique when inquiry and observation are insufficient:

Answer: Reperformance (actually performing the control in real time)

15. External evidence is more reliable than internal evidence because:

Answer: Independence of the provider — external sources are independent

16. Which continuous auditing technique is most suitable when regular processing cannot be interrupted?

Answer: SCARF/EAM (embedded in host application, monitoring selectively)

17. AI/ML results in IS audit must ALWAYS be:

Answer: Interpreted at some point by a person

18. Stop-or-go sampling is used when:

Answer: The IS auditor believes relatively few errors will be found in a population

19. Discovery sampling is most appropriate when:

Answer: The objective is to seek out fraud, irregularities or circumvention of regulations

20. Variable sampling is primarily used in:

Answer: Substantive testing (deals with monetary values and quantities)

KEY CONCEPT RECAP

The "Big 5" Concepts Always Tested:

  1. CSA primary benefit = Management ownership/reinforcement of internal controls
  2. Audit risk = Inherent + Control + Detection; Higher materiality = Lower acceptable audit risk
  3. Audit charter = Establishes authority for ALL audit activities; approved by Board/Audit Committee
  4. Risk-based audit approach = Most critical step = Risk assessment; focus on business processes
  5. Control classifications = Preventive (strongest) → Deterrent → Detective → Corrective → Compensating

The "Must Know" Relationships:

  • More controls → Less substantive testing required
  • Higher confidence coefficient → Larger sample size
  • Higher materiality → Lower acceptable audit risk
  • External evidence → More reliable than internal
  • Reperformance → Most reliable evidence technique

Common Confusions:

Often Confused Clarification
Audit Charter vs. Engagement Letter Charter = ALL audits; Letter = ONE specific audit
CSA vs. Internal Audit CSA enhances audit; does NOT replace it
Continuous Auditing vs. Continuous Monitoring CA = IS auditor function; CM = Management function
Compliance Testing vs. Substantive Testing Compliance = Are controls working?; Substantive = Is data accurate?
Inherent Risk vs. Control Risk Inherent = No controls considered; Control = Controls fail to prevent
IS vs. IT IS = IT + People + Process; IT = Just the technology
Corrective vs. Detective vs. Preventive Backup = Corrective (restores); IDS = Detective (detects); MFA = Preventive (prevents)

MNEMONICS & MEMORY AIDS

1. ISACA Standards Hierarchy

"SGT (Sergeant) leads the way!" - Standards (Mandatory — Sergeant's orders) - Guidelines (Guidance — follow the sergeant's advice) - Tools & Techniques (Examples — optional tools)

2. Audit Process Phases

"PFR" — Please Follow Rules - Planning - Fieldwork/Documentation - Reporting/Follow-Up

3. Types of Audit Risk

"I Can Detect Overall" - Inherent risk - Control risk - Detection risk - Overall audit risk

4. Control Classifications (in order of occurrence)

"Pretty Dumb Dogs Can Compensate" - Preventive - Deterrent - Detective - Corrective - Compensating

5. ISACA Code of Ethics

"SPSMIC-E" - Support standards - Perform with objectivity - Serve stakeholders lawfully - Maintain privacy/confidentiality - Inform appropriate parties - Competency — maintain it - Education — support stakeholder learning

6. Continuous Auditing Techniques

"SSAIC" — Some Systems Always Integrate Continuously - SCARF/EAM - Snapshots - Audit Hooks - ITF (Integrated Test Facility) - CIS (Continuous and Intermittent Simulation)

7. Audit Report Components (6 Objectives)

"FRARSAP" — Find Reliable Audit Results and Share All Positives - Formally present results - Record (serve as formal closure) - Assurance (provide statements of assurance) - Reference (valued reference for researchers) - Subsequent audit (basis for follow-up) - Promotion (promote audit credibility)

8. Evidence Reliability Order (Most → Least Reliable)

"REOPI" — Really Excellent Objective People Inform - Reperformance (best) - External (sources) - Objective evidence - Personnel (qualified) - Internal sources (least)

9. Sampling Size Relationship

"CEEP" — Confidence is Expensive, Errors are Proportional - Confidence coefficient ↑ → Sample size ↑ - Expected error rate ↑ → Sample size ↑ - Error tolerance (tolerable error rate) ↑ → Sample size ↓ - Precision ↑ → Sample size ↓

10. Risk-Based Audit Approach

"ESCO" — Everyone Should Control Outcomes" - Evaluate risk candidates - Schedule audits based on risk ratings - Conduct compliance or substantive testing - Opinion/conclusion on control environment
